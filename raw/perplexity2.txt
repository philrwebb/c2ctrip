You’re almost certainly thinking of the **Coast to Coast Walk (C2C)**, the famous 190–192 mile (about 309 km) trail from **St Bees on the Irish Sea in Cumbria** to **Robin Hood’s Bay on the North Sea in North Yorkshire** through the Lake District, Yorkshire Dales, and North York Moors.[1][2][3]

Below is a practical, mid‑range itinerary and the best time to walk it.

***

### Best time to walk

Most walkers do the C2C between **late April and early October**, with the **sweet spot in May–June** for:
- Warmer, drier weather and fewer thunderstorms than July–August.[3][4]
- Lush greenery without the summer crowds and peak‑season prices.[5][3]

Avoid:
- **Winter** (slippery, short days, some B&Bs closed).[4][6]
- **Late March / early April** if you’re not experienced with cold, wet, and possible snow patches in the Lake District tops.[6][3]

***

### Typical 14‑day itinerary (St Bees → Robin Hood’s Bay)

Most guided‑holiday operators parcel it into **13 walking days plus arrival/departure**, but you can compress or stretch this.[7][8][4]

1. **Day 1 – St Bees → Ennerdale Bridge**  
   - Start at St Bees, dip your boots in the Irish Sea, then head into the southern Lake District.  
   - ~18–20 km via High Stile ridge (bag some Wainwright fells if you like).[3][6]

2. **Day 2 – Ennerdale Bridge → Wasdale Head**  
   - Deep‑valley walking with views of Scafell Pike and surrounding fells.  
   - ~16–18 km, steep but relatively short.[6]

3. **Day 3 – Wasdale Head → Rosthwaite (Borrowdale)**  
   - Crossing the central Lakes via Sty Head and Dungeon Ghyll.  
   - ~18–20 km; the classic “tough but rewarding” day.[3][6]

4. **Day 4 – Rosthwaite → Patterdale**  
   - Along Grasmere and onto the eastern side of the central Lakes.  
   - ~18–20 km with some ups and downs.[6][3]

5. **Day 5 – Patterdale → Pooley Bridge**  
   - Around Ullswater, then into the Eden Valley.  
   - ~16–18 km, more gentler walking.[3][6]

6. **Day 6 – Pooley Bridge → Brough / Appleby**  
   - Into the fringes of the Yorkshire Dales with rolling farmland.  
   - ~18–22 km depending on where you stop.[8][6]

7. **Day 7 – Brough → Kirkby Stephen**  
   - Cross the Pennines into the Dales proper.  
   - ~16–18 km; not too long if you want an easy evening.[8][6]

8. **Day 8 – Kirkby Stephen → Keld**  
   - Classic Dales scenery; quiet villages and limestone gorges.  
   - ~18–20 km, moderate ascent.[9][6]

9. **Day 9 – Keld → Reeth**  
   - Around Swaledale, often considered one of the prettiest stretches.  
   - ~16–18 km.[9][6]

10. **Day 10 – Reeth → Sutton Bank / Brompton‑on‑Swale**  
    - Up onto the North York Moors edge, often via Sutton Bank.  
    - ~22–25 km; one of the longer days.[9][6]

11. **Day 11 – Brompton‑on‑Swale → Blakey Ridge**  
    - Into the Moors proper, with wide‑open heather and moorland.  
    - ~20–25 km, depending on B&B location.[6][9]

12. **Day 12 – Blakey Ridge → Littlebeck / Egton Bridge**  
    - Steep climbs and descents around the Esk Valley.  
    - ~18–22 km.[9][6]

13. **Day 13 – Littlebeck / Egton Bridge → Robin Hood’s Bay**  
    - Final moorland and coastal‑cliff walk into the village.  
    - ~20–26 km, with a steep final descent into the bay.  
    - Finish by dipping your boots in the North Sea near the Coast‑to‑Coast monument.[7][3][6]

14. **Day 14 – Celebrate / depart from Robin Hood’s Bay**  
    - No walking day; you can explore the village, catch a train/bus, or spend an extra night.[4][7]

***

### If you want a shorter schedule

Some people do it in **12 days** (slightly longer average days) or **self‑guided in 10–11 days** if you’re comfortable with 25–30 km days.  Conversely, you can spread it over **16–18 days** for very gentle days and more rest.[10][7][8][9]

***

### Quick logistics tips

- Public transport: Trains and buses serve key towns (Keswick, Appleby, Kirkby Stephen, Reeth, Sutton Bank, Guisborough, Whitby/Robin Hood’s Bay).[4][6]
- B&Bs & luggage transfer: Many operators offer luggage‑transfer services, allowing you to carry only a day‑pack.[11][7][8]
