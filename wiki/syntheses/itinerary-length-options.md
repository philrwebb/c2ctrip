## Comparison

| Option        | Typical profile                                             | Daily pattern                                                       | Main tradeoff                                     |
| ------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| 10 days       | Strong walkers comfortable with 20 to 25 km days            | Longer average distances, but still with shorter hard mountain days | Faster completion with less margin for fatigue    |
| 14 days       | Mainstream self-guided or supported walkers                 | Moderate stages with more recovery built in                         | More nights and logistics, but lower daily stress |
| 16 to 18 days | Walkers prioritizing comfort, sightseeing, or lower fatigue | Shorter, easier stages with more flexibility                        | Simplest pacing, but longest overall trip         |

## Analysis

Across the four sources, the real difference is not disagreement about the route but different assumptions about walker strength and desired comfort. [[summaries/perplexity1]] and [[summaries/perplexity2]] describe the standard 14-day framing, [[summaries/perplexity3]] shows how the same route can be compressed for a group that already knows it can manage 20 to 25 km days, and [[summaries/notebookllm]] adds detail for 14 to 16 stage operational planning. The western mountain terrain remains the main constraint in every version, while some lowland sections still need careful stage splitting to avoid overlong days.

## Recommendations

- Prefer 10 days only when the group already knows it can sustain strong mileage and recover from consecutive hard days.
- Prefer 14 days as the default planning baseline for first-time or mixed-ability groups.
- Prefer 16 to 18 days when comfort, sightseeing time, or conservative pacing matters more than finishing quickly.
- Add baggage support if the group wants to keep a faster schedule without carrying overnight loads.
