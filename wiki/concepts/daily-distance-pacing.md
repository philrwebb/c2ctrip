## Definition

Daily distance pacing is the practice of matching each walking stage on the [[entities/coast-to-coast-walk]] to the group's comfort range while accounting for terrain and recovery needs.

## How It Works

The sources treat distance as only part of the pacing problem. A strong group can complete the route in about 10 to 12 days if it is comfortable with high daily mileage, but hard mountain crossings still justify shorter stages. Mainstream plans of 14 to 15 days reduce average stress by spreading the route into moderate segments, while 16 to 18 day plans lower intensity further for comfort-focused walkers.

## Key Parameters

- Comfortable daily distance range
- Elevation gain and technical difficulty
- Back-to-back hard-day tolerance
- Availability of short recovery stages or rest days
- Group size and shared pace expectations
- Whether historically overlong segments are split into two days

## When To Use

Use this concept when choosing between a 10-day, 12-day, 14-day, or longer schedule and when deciding where to place shorter recovery days.

## Risks & Pitfalls

- Planning by average kilometers alone and ignoring terrain severity
- Stacking multiple hard days without recovery
- Adopting a fast itinerary before confirming every walker can actually sustain it
- Leaving lowland crossing days too long because they look easier on paper
