## Definition

Trail accessibility and multiuse design is the set of route and infrastructure choices that broaden who can use the [[entities/coast-to-coast-walk]], including walkers with mobility constraints, pushchair users in suitable sections, cyclists, and equestrians.

## How It Works

The source highlights accessibility audits, wayfinding readability upgrades, stile replacement with gates, targeted surface improvements in less rugged sections, and a parallel riders route using bridleways and quiet lanes. These interventions separate mode-specific traffic where needed and reduce avoidable barriers.

## Key Parameters

- Physical barriers (stiles, steps, narrow bridges)
- Surface type and durability by terrain
- Signage clarity and legibility
- Segregation between pedestrian and rider flows
- Suitability limits in steep or highly technical sections

## When To Use

Use this concept when planning inclusive access improvements, evaluating route suitability for mixed user types, or balancing universal-design goals with upland terrain constraints.

## Risks & Pitfalls

- Applying a one-size-fits-all accessibility standard to terrain that is inherently varied
- Improving one user mode while creating conflicts for another
- Treating accessibility upgrades as complete without considering transport and accommodation access at stage ends
